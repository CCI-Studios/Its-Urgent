What is OAB?

Overactive Bladder (OAB) is a symptomatic condition marked by the sudden, compelling desire to pass urinei. This sudden urge to urinate, which is difficult to suppress, can lead to the involuntary loss of urine (incontinence).ii

An estimated 2.9 million Canadian men and women suffer from OAB.iii

OAB symptoms occur in approximately equal numbers of men and women, and OAB symptoms are more widespread in older patients of either gender.iv Urge incontinence is the most common form of urinary incontinence in older adults, but this incontinence is not an inevitable consequence of aging and therefore can, and should, be treated.v

What Causes OAB?

Overactive bladder occurs when the bladder’s smooth muscle, known as the detrusor muscle, involuntarily contracts, which creates the urgent need to urinate.vi Rushing to make it to the bathroom in time is common for people with overactive bladder.

What are OAB Symptoms?vii

  • A strong, sudden urge to urinate
  • The need to urinate more often than usual during the day, usually more than eight times in a 24 hour period
  • Waking up two or more times in the night to urinate (which is referred to as nocturia)
  • Experience urge incontinence, the involuntary loss of urine associated with urgency to urinate

How is OAB Treated?

Upon diagnosis, non-drug, lifestyle and behaviour modification strategies are first used to manage OAB.viii These include caffeine reduction, fluid management, bladder retraining and journaling whereby patients keep a bladder diary for a few days to document when, how much and what kind of fluids they consume, when they urinate, whether they feel an urge to urinate and whether they experience incontinence (loss of urine).ix

The following are general recommendations to help with incontinence of all causes, including urge incontinence due to OAB.

Dietary restrictions: the following food and drinks may cause increased bladder sensitivity and symptoms, and therefore should be reduced or eliminated altogether:x

  • All alcoholic beverages
  • All caffeinated beverages (coffee, tea, etc.)
  • All sodas and carbonated beverages (even diet sodas)
  • Citrus fruits – lemons, limes and oranges
  • Pineapple, strawberries, tomatoes (particularly anything with tomato paste or concentrate)
  • Spicy foodsxi
  • Vinegarxii, orange juicexiii or cranberry juice

Fluid management: record the volume of fluid you drink in a day and well as when you drink themxiv. Try to drink most of your fluids before 7:00 PM to reduce the frequency of nighttime waking.xv

Bladder retraining: Also known as urge suppression. Remain calm, stop what you are doing and squeeze your rectal muscles 10-15 times quickly and try to delay going to the bathroom by a few minutes.xvi

Behavioural techniques alone might not be enough to manage the symptoms related to OAB, in which case prescription medication may be required. It is important to discuss all symptoms related to OAB with your doctor.

In fact, the chances of treatment success are better when behavioural techniques and drug therapies are used in combination.xvii

Medication

OAB is characterized by abnormal bladder contractions, which makes you want to urinate even when your bladder isn’t full. A class of drugs called anticholinergic agents block the action of a chemical messenger – acetylcholine – that sends the signals that trigger these contractions.xviii Stopping the bladder muscle from contracting too soon can result in the bladder filling completely, resulting in less urgency, fewer accidents and fewer trips to the bathroom.

There are several anticholinergic medications available in Canada; however, the only medication available through B.C. PharmaCare for the treatment of OAB is an older drug known to have significant side effects, including dry mouth and constipation, which are significant and well-recognized factors that may lead individuals to discontinue treatment.xix

What are the Effects of OAB?

OAB can greatly impact patients’ lives. Patients who have incontinence as a result of their OAB symptoms experience an even greater impact on their quality of life than those who do not experience incontinence.xx

For example, OAB might lead someone to:

  • Limit social activity: They may stop participating in the things they enjoy, such as going out, to avoid potential embarrassment due to loss of bladder control in public.xxi
  • Experience a disruption in sleep due to getting up frequently during the night to go to the bathroom.xxii
  • Affect sexual intimacy, either because of embarrassment or feelings of being unattractive, or due to fear of possible urinary leakage.xxiii
  • Experience depression.xxiv
  • Experience loss of control and self-esteem.xxv
